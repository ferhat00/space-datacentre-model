## How the methods combine

Viability is a *conjunction*, so the methods stack rather than compete. **Physics-based subsystem sizing** turns an IT-power target into mass, area and cost; **parametric cost / CERs** and **levelized-cost (LCOC/TCO)** turn those into a $/compute number; the **communication-intensity (Γ) gate** and **orbital-edge-computing** models decide which workloads can actually run; **break-even / trade-space** finds the price or design point of parity; and **sensitivity** + **Monte-Carlo** quantify how much the verdict depends on the most contested inputs. The repo implements all of these over one calibrated kernel (`odc/`), so the app, notebook and dashboard agree by construction.

## 1. Levelized Cost of Compute / Total Cost of Ownership (LCOC/TCO)

*A capital-recovery-factor levelization that converts the full lifecycle capex+opex of an orbital (or terrestrial) compute asset into a per-unit price of delivered compute ($/GPU-hr, $/PFLOP-hr, $/kWh, $/token), with separate amortization schedules for short-life IT, short-life satellites, and long-life ground facilities.*

TCO is the sum of all costs to build, launch, operate, insure, and dispose of a compute asset over its life. LCOC is TCO converted to a unit price of *delivered* compute by levelizing capex with a capital recovery factor (CRF) and dividing annualized cost by the compute actually sold. The CRF is CRF = r(1+r)^n / [(1+r)^n - 1], where r is WACC and n is the asset life in years. This is the discounted-cashflow annuity that spreads a lump-sum capex into an equal annual charge — the same machinery as LCOE for power plants. The repo implements exactly this in odc/core.py (crf() at line 121; lcoc_and_npv() at line 201).

The defining feature for orbital data centres is MIXED ASSET LIVES. SemiAnalysis levelizes three streams separately: the whole orbital station on a short life (5 yr, extending to 10 yr post-2032) at a space WACC; ground IT on a 5 yr life at ground WACC; and the ground facility shell on a 15 yr life at ground WACC. The repo mirrors this (core.py lines 214-219): crf_s on the station, crf_git on 5-yr IT, crf_gfc on 15-yr facility. The life mismatch — 5-yr orbital station vs 15-yr terrestrial facility, the latter partly forced by the FCC 5-year LEO deorbit rule (effective 29 Sep 2024) — is the single largest driver of the gap: SemiAnalysis reports monthly levelized datacentre capital ~18x higher in space, and the repo's provenance note (provenance.py line 53) attributes the ~17-18x levelized ratio to life mismatch, not launch (launch is the largest absolute capex LINE but not the largest levelized driver).

WACC is the most fragile input. SemiAnalysis uses 10.3% terrestrial (7% pre-tax debt, 20% cost of equity, 75/25 debt/equity) and 15.0% initial for space declining to 10.3% over ~10 years as risk de-risks. The repo encodes this (core.py lines 105-106) and flags wacc_space as "MOST FRAGILE param", with a dedicated odc/finance.py providing wacc_trajectory() and a non_financeable_case() at 20%+ WACC reflecting that orbital assets are not repossessable collateral.

The TCO→LCOC conversion adds two grossing-up steps SemiAnalysis applies: a radiation/availability factor (95% space vs 100% ground) and redundancy provisioning (20% spare GPUs in space vs 5% ground, since in-orbit repair is impossible). Together these are a "26% gross up" that turns a 2026 space TCO of $8.64/hr/GPU into an LCOC of $10.91/hr/GPU (vs ground LCOC $2.49 from TCO $2.37). Unit conventions chain off the per-kW levelized cost: $/GPU-hr = LCOC × kW/GPU; $/PFLOP-hr = $/GPU-hr ÷ dense PFLOPS/GPU; $/token follows from tokens-per-PFLOP-hr. The repo computes all of these (core.py lines 249-255) and notably charges only SOLD hours (multiplies by utilization, lines 248-250) rather than burying utilization in the divisor — a methodological choice that differs slightly from a pure SemiAnalysis read.

**Ambitious read.** Read optimistically, LCOC/TCO is the method that shows orbital compute CONVERGING to terrestrial. SemiAnalysis's own levelization has the space-to-ground LCOC ratio at ~4.4x in 2026, falling to ~30% premium in the early 2030s and reaching parity around 2040 in the base case (earlier in the "Elon Musk" binding-constraint scenario). The mechanism is launch-cost learning ($1,400-1,800/kg Falcon 9 -> ~$250/kg Starship) plus extending orbital asset life from 5 to 10 years post-2032, which roughly halves the dominant levelized-capital penalty. Google's Project Suncatcher reframes the same TCO logic as launched-power-price: at ~$200/kg-LEO-equivalent (achievable ~mid-2030s with ~180 Starship launches/yr) the amortized launched power price is ~$810/kW/yr, squarely inside the terrestrial ~$570-3,000/kW/yr band — and orbital solar in dawn-dusk SSO is up to 8x more productive with near-continuous illumination, attacking the opex side of TCO. Starcloud's pitch ($0.05/kWh-class energy, 40 MW Starship-packaged modules scaling to GW clusters) and Thales ASCEND (Europe by 2036, large CO2 savings) round out the bull case: once launch and life inputs cross their thresholds, the same levelization that condemns 2026 economics flips to favour orbit, and the marginal energy/cooling cost in space trends toward zero.

**Skeptical read.** The same method, fed defensible space-systems-engineering inputs, refuses to close. Turyshev (arXiv:2604.27197) runs the levelization backwards: a 10-40 k$/kW terrestrial benchmark permits only 250-1,000 $/kg for combined launch-plus-build, which is 3.4-13.5x BELOW today's Falcon 9 dedicated LEO price — and that is before the radiator and PV mass burden of 34-59 kg/kW (vs his 29.4 kg/kW for PV+storage+radiator alone). Four inputs the bull case treats as solved are not: (1) Mass/power — flight-demonstrated iROSA is ~75 W/kg, and high-flux radiators rejecting hundreds of W/m^2 at GW scale have NOT been flight-demonstrated; SemiAnalysis's own ISS reference is 70 kW over 325 m^2 (~215 W/m^2) on 30-year-old tech, and the 2.50e3 m^2 radiator per MW in Turyshev is a launched-mass anchor that dwarfs optimistic projections. (2) Life mismatch — the FCC 5-year LEO deorbit rule pins orbital asset life at ~5 yr against a 15-yr terrestrial facility, producing the ~18x monthly levelized-capital penalty; the bull case's "extend to 10 yr post-2032" is an assumption, not a demonstrated capability. (3) Financeability — orbital assets are not repossessable, not refinanceable, and depreciate unpredictably (Baillie Gifford via Bloomberg), so the correct WACC is plausibly the 20%+ "non-financeable" case the repo models (finance.py), not the 15%->10.3% glide path; since LCOC is roughly linear in CRF, a 20% vs 10.3% WACC alone can swing the headline by ~1.5-2x. (4) Utilization/comms ceiling — Turyshev shows closure only for low Earth-coupled communication intensity, high effective utilization, and long delivered life simultaneously; general terrestrial-user compute does not close. Honest verdict: the method is sound; the GW bull case depends on several un-demonstrated W/m^2, kg/kW, asset-life, and WACC inputs, any one of which can break parity.

**In this model.** This repo IS an implementation of the SemiAnalysis LCOC/TCO method, and the literature maps directly onto its parameters. The core engine (odc/core.py) levelizes with crf() (line 121) and lcoc_and_npv() (line 201), using exactly the mixed-life structure the dossier describes: crf_s on the short-life station, crf_git on 5-yr IT, crf_gfc on the 15-yr ground facility (lines 214-219), then derives $/GPU-hr, $/PFLOP-hr from per-kW LCOC (lines 249-255). The WACC inputs (wacc_space=0.150 -> 0.103, wacc_ground=0.103) at core.py:105-106 match SemiAnalysis exactly, and odc/finance.py already operationalizes the two most important skeptic levers: wacc_trajectory() for the de-risking glide path and non_financeable_case() at 20%+ WACC for the non-repossessable-collateral argument (Bloomberg/Baillie Gifford). odc/provenance.py:69 hard-codes the verified SemiAnalysis LCOC anchors (gpu_hr_s=10.91, gpu_hr_g=2.49, with the TCO-vs-LCOC distinction noted: $8.64/$2.37), so the repo correctly stores LCOC not TCO. ## 2. Communication-intensity / downlink-ceiling (Gamma) gate

*A feasibility gate that compares a workload's communication intensity (data that must be moved to ground per kWh of IT energy, GB/kWh) against a power-dependent downlink ceiling (Turyshev's Gamma_max), declaring a workload "compute-bound" and closeable in orbit only if its Gamma demand sits under the ceiling, and "link-bound" (structurally infeasible regardless of compute economics) if it does not.*

The method defines communication intensity Gamma = D_sg / E_IT, the bidirectional space-to-ground data exchanged per unit of IT energy (bit/J, or in engineering units GB/kWh). On the supply side, Turyshev (arXiv:2604.27197, 2026) derives a sustained communication ceiling Gamma_max = (N_term * eta_link * f_vis * p_avail * f_sched * R_pk) / (U * P_IT), where N_term is the number of downlink terminals, eta_link link efficiency, f_vis ground-station visibility fraction, p_avail link availability (cloud-cover limited for optical), f_sched scheduling fraction, R_pk the peak physical-layer downlink rate, U utilization, and P_IT the IT electrical power. The decisive structural feature is that Gamma_max scales as P_IT^-1: a fixed downlink budget is spread over more compute as the node grows, so the data-per-energy ceiling falls inversely with power. For a representative configuration this anchors at roughly 148 GB/kWh at 0.1 MW, 14.8 GB/kWh at 1 MW, and 1.48 GB/kWh at 10 MW.

The gate itself is a single comparison: a workload closes in orbit only if its required Gamma (its egress-data-per-IT-energy) is <= Gamma_max(P_IT). Equivalently, headroom = Gamma_max / Gamma_demand; >1 means compute-bound (feed-able, the accelerators are the binding resource), <1 means link-bound (the downlink/ISL cannot evacuate results fast enough and idle compute is the binding resource). Because the ceiling drops with power, the same workload that closes at 1 MW can become link-bound at 10-100 MW, which is exactly the regime the gigawatt-constellation proposals target.

Inputs are therefore split cleanly: the platform supplies R_pk, terminal count, ground-network availability, and P_IT; the workload supplies its intrinsic Gamma. The intellectual lineage is Bhattacherjee et al. "In-orbit Computing" (HotNets 2020) and Denby & Lucia (ASPLOS 2020), which established that for Earth-observation and similar pipelines, downlink/ground-station bandwidth — not on-board compute — is the historically binding constraint, motivating in-orbit processing to shrink egress data. Turyshev formalizes the same intuition into a power-scaling ceiling and ties it to a closing condition.

Outputs are a per-workload feasibility verdict and a maximum link-feasible IT power. The companion economic test in Turyshev is the closing condition: terrestrial-user general compute closes only for (i) low Earth-coupled communication intensity, (ii) high effective utilization, (iii) long delivered lifetime, and (iv) very low combined launch-plus-build cost. Quantitatively, matching a terrestrial 10-40 k$/kW infrastructure benchmark allows only 250-1000 $/kg for combined launch plus spacecraft build — which the paper notes is 3.4-13.5x below the current public Falcon 9 dedicated-LEO launch price, before any build cost. The mass term that drives this is 29.4 kg/kW for PV + storage + radiator, rising to 34-59 kg/kW (~40 kg/kW representative) once fixed spacecraft mass is included.

**Ambitious read.** Read optimistically, the Gamma gate is not a blocker but a workload-selection map that the leading programs have already internalized. The ceiling is highest exactly where the revenue-bearing orbital workloads sit: frontier training and batch/offline inference move very little data per kWh (datasets are pre-positioned once; only weights/checkpoints, ~0.7-0.9 TB FP8, and async results leave orbit), so their intrinsic Gamma is far below the ceiling even at multi-MW nodes. Google Suncatcher attacks the supply side of the inequality directly: 1.6 Tbps demonstrated per transceiver pair and a tens-of-Tbps DWDM target per link mean inter-satellite bandwidth large enough to make a constellation behave as one logical cluster, so compute can be co-located with data and only distilled outputs are downlinked — collapsing the egress term. Thales ASCEND's EU-funded study concludes 10 MW-class space data centres are technically, economically and environmentally feasible and targets ~1 GW by 2050, and Starcloud (Nvidia-backed, trained a model in orbit in late 2025) is pushing deployable low-mass radiators as core IP. The physics tailwinds are real: orbital solar is up to ~8x as productive as ground and near-continuous in dawn-dusk SSO, no water cooling, and launch projected below $200/kg by the mid-2030s on full Starship reuse — which is precisely the regime where Turyshev's own closing condition (250-1000 $/kg) flips from impossible to plausible. In this reading the Gamma gate simply tells you to run training and batch inference in orbit and keep latency-chat on the ground, which is what the serious actors already propose.

**Skeptical read.** The skeptical read is that the Gamma gate's P_IT^-1 scaling is fatal precisely at the scale the bull case needs. The ceiling is ~14.8 GB/kWh at 1 MW but only ~1.48 GB/kWh at 10 MW and ~0.15 GB/kWh at 100 MW: gigawatt constellations would have to move data per unit energy at rates that no demonstrated downlink network approaches, and bisection bandwidth across a large constellation (not just point-to-point ISL) is an unquantified gap even in the Suncatcher work. The supply-side demos are bench-scale single-pair (1.6 Tbps on a lab transceiver), not a flight-proven mesh, and optical downlink is cloud-cover limited, needing 4+ ground stations just to reach ~98% availability. The economics compound this: Turyshev's closing condition demands 250-1000 $/kg combined launch-plus-build — 3.4-13.5x below today's Falcon 9 — against a mass budget of 34-59 kg/kW, and the single least-mature subsystem, the radiator, has no GW-scale W/m2 heat-rejection flight demonstration; radiators at ~14-17 kg/m2 dominate mass and are far heavier than solar. Power generation itself is anchored by reality, not aspiration: iROSA delivers ~75 W/kg, orders below the specific power the mass budgets implicitly assume. Then the financing wall: LEO assets face a 5-year FCC deorbit life (vs a ~15-year terrestrial facility), are non-repossessable, non-serviceable, and uninsurable at GW scale (single-risk capacity ~$300-325M against $10B+ assets), so the Cape Town Space Protocol does not apply and no bank can lend against them — leaving a pure-equity WACC near 20% rather than the SemiAnalysis 15%->10.3% trajectory. A high cost of capital amortized over a 5-year life against a 15-year terrestrial competitor is the quiet killer the Gamma gate's optimistic workload-selection story tends to skip.

**In this model.** For orbital-data-centre viability, the Gamma gate is the demand-side feasibility filter that must be passed before any economics matter: it answers "can the link evacuate this workload's output at this node power?" independently of whether the compute pays. Applied correctly it partitions the workload space — high-Gamma latency chat/agents (~40 GB/kWh) and embeddings are structurally link-bound and belong on the ground; low-Gamma frontier training (~0.5 GB/kWh) and batch inference (~4 GB/kWh) are the only workloads that close in orbit, and even those flip to link-bound as node power climbs because the ceiling falls as 1/P. This repo already implements the method directly. odc/workloads.py encodes the ceiling as gamma_ceiling_gb_per_kwh(p_it_mw) = 14.8 / p_it_mw with anchors 148/14.8/1.48 GB/kWh at 0.1/1/10 MW, per-workload gamma_gb_per_kwh values, and the gate functions closes_in_orbit() and gamma_headroom(). odc/comms.py couples this ceiling to concrete link budgets (MATURE_2026 Starlink-class ~100 Gbps/terminal; SUNCATCHER_TARGET ~10 Tbps ISL / 200 Gbps downlink, 6 ground stations) via downlink_feasible_mw() and a network_availability model (1 - (1-clear_sky)^stations). The honest caveats to carry: (1) the 14.8/P_MW closed form is a single-config anchor, not a universal law — it should be re-derived from the platform's actual N_term, R_pk and availability per scenario; (2) bisection bandwidth across a large cluster is unmodelled and is the real risk for training-as-one-logical-cluster; (3) the gate is necessary but not sufficient — it must be run in series with the mass (34-59 kg/kW), 5-year-life, radiator-W/m2, and non-financeable-WACC gates in odc/finance.py, because a workload can pass Gamma and still fail on $/kg or cost of capital. Recommended use: treat Gamma headroom <1 as a hard reject, and headroom >1 as a license to proceed to the economic gates, never as a viability verdict on its own.

## 3. Orbital-edge-computing (OEC) modelling

*A systems-modelling method that treats a satellite (or constellation) as a computer whose performance is gated by the coupling of onboard energy harvest, compute throughput, and the downlink/contact-window bottleneck — deciding what to process at the space edge versus downlink raw.*

OEC modelling, formalised by Denby & Lucia (ASPLOS 2020), reframes an Earth-observing nanosatellite constellation as a distributed computer in which the binding constraint is not compute but the mismatch between data generation rate, onboard energy, and the downlink available during brief ground-station contacts. The model is operationalised in their `cote` simulator/runtime, which "models orbital mechanics and Earth rotation to track ground station and satellite positions over time" and "models data collection along each satellite ground track, as well as the energy and latency of sensing, computing, and communication for an entire constellation." Core kinematic primitives: the ground track is decomposed into ground track frames (GTFs); the ground track frame rate (GTFR) is the rate at which an entirely new, non-overlapping scene fills the camera view, and its inverse GTFP is the time to traverse one frame. To minimise data volume, the model captures at the GTFR (no redundant frames), so total data per revolution is fixed by geometry (altitude, ground-sample-distance/GSD, swath) independent of how many satellites share the work.

The downlink/contact-window sub-model is the load-bearing piece: the paper shows a bent-pipe (downlink-all-raw) architecture "breaks down as constellation population increases" because a ground station "optimistically and ideally positioned to observe every pass... supports only 9 satellites per revolution," so a 1,000-satellite constellation would need 112 ideally-placed stations — and most downlinked images contain no features of interest. OEC's answer is to process at the edge (e.g. DetectNet/CNN building-footprint detection on 0.3 m/px SpaceNet imagery) and downlink only results, which the paper reports reduces ground infrastructure over 24x versus bent-pipe.

The energy/compute sub-model is intermittency-aware. CubeSats relying on surface-mounted panels collect "a few watts"; storage is small (the paper focuses on supercapacitors over heated batteries to avoid the -40C thermal-heating power overhead). A computational nanosatellite pipeline (CNP) parallelises collection and processing across satellites by geographic position without cross-link coordination; increasing "pipeline depth" raises aggregate parallel work and aggregate harvested energy per revolution. When harvested energy < energy required to process all data, adding satellites increases coverage (fraction of GTFs captured); when energy suffices, full coverage is possible but can still be missed due to processing latency — yielding the reported >617x edge-processing-latency reduction from pipelining. Kodan (ASPLOS 2023) extends this to the downstream "computational bottleneck": after edge filtering shifts the bottleneck from downlink to onboard compute, Kodan generates specialised ML models and a runtime that trades geospatial precision for speed to maximise "data value density" of a saturated downlink (reported +89-97%).

The same constraint stack — energy harvest under eclipse, fixed downlink during contacts, communication-intensity per unit of compute energy — is what later gigawatt-scale orbital-data-centre (ODC) analyses generalise. Turyshev (arXiv, 2026) recasts the OEC downlink bottleneck as a communication-intensity ceiling Gamma (usable GB moved per kWh of IT energy, scaling ~1/power) and closes seven simultaneous constraints (PV generation, eclipse recharge, radiative heat rejection, space-to-ground comms, utilisation, replacement cadence, compute-years over life) to derive deployed-mass-per-IT-power and economic break-even conditions.

**Ambitious read.** Read optimistically, OEC is the proof-of-concept that the orbital-compute thesis rests on. Denby & Lucia showed quantitatively (>24x ground-infrastructure reduction, >617x latency reduction) that moving compute to the space edge beats bent-pipe downlink once a constellation is large — i.e. the binding constraint (downlink) can be engineered around by computing in orbit. Kodan then showed the residual onboard-compute bottleneck is itself tractable (+89-97% data value density). Scaled up, this is the Starcloud / Suncatcher / ASCEND bull case: in a dawn-dusk SSO the sun is available ~99% of the year and a panel is up to ~8x more productive than on Earth (Google), so power is "near-limitless and free." Free-space optics already hit 1.6 Tbps on a bench (Google), attacking the very downlink bottleneck OEC identified. TPU memory survived ~3x its shielded 5-year dose in accelerator testing. If launch falls to ~$200-250/kg (Starship), SemiAnalysis sees space within ~30% of terrestrial cost by the early 2030s and at parity ~2040, and Thales/EU's ASCEND argues a 1 GW orbital tier is feasible by 2050 with net-zero benefits and data sovereignty. In this reading, OEC's energy-compute-downlink co-design is exactly the modelling discipline that makes gigawatt constellations a scheduling-and-cost-down problem rather than a physics impossibility.

**Skeptical read.** The skeptical space-systems read is that OEC's own logic, scaled, indicts the gigawatt vision. OEC was validated for low-power, latency-insensitive Earth-observation filtering on a few-watt cubesat — precisely the "space-native preprocessing / communications-integrated edge compute" regime Turyshev calls credible. General terrestrial-user AI compute is the opposite: high communication intensity, high power, latency-sensitive. Solar specific power is bounded by reality: iROSA flies at ~75 W/kg, so gigawatt arrays imply enormous mass even before radiators and structure. The 5-year FCC-driven deorbit life (chips/orbit) versus a 15-year terrestrial facility is a ~3x amortisation penalty that SemiAnalysis translates into ~17x higher levelized cost today and a ~4.4x LCOC gap ($10.91 vs $2.49/hr/GPU). ASCEND declares feasibility without publishing the model. The honest verdict: OEC modelling is sound and confirmed; the gigawatt extrapolation is unverified on radiator W/m2 at scale, optimistic on mass, and not yet financeable.

## 4. Monte-Carlo / probabilistic uncertainty quantification

*A method that propagates input-parameter uncertainty (encoded as triangular/PERT/lognormal distributions over low/most-likely/high ranges) through a deterministic system-and-cost model via repeated random sampling, producing a probability distribution (S-curve) of outcomes and confidence levels rather than a single point estimate.*

The core idea: replace each uncertain scalar input with a probability distribution, draw N random samples (typically 1,000-100,000) jointly across all inputs, evaluate the deterministic model once per draw, and accumulate the output samples into an empirical distribution. From that distribution you read percentiles (P10/P50/P90), confidence levels (the probability that cost <= target), and a cumulative "S-curve." Inputs are most commonly parameterised from three-point estimates (a = optimistic, m = most-likely, b = pessimistic). The PERT/beta variant gives expected value E = (a + 4m + b)/6 and standard deviation SD = (b - a)/6, weighting the mode heavily; the triangular variant weights all three equally and is the conservative default when the shape is unknown. Correlation between cost elements (and between cost and schedule) is imposed via a correlation matrix so that a bad draw on one driver tends to co-move with related drivers, which materially fattens the tail versus naive independent sampling.

In NASA practice this is formalised as the Joint Cost and Schedule Confidence Level (JCL) analysis (NASA Cost Estimating Handbook Appendix J; NPR 7120.5 requires a JCL for projects above the cost threshold). JCL runs a Monte Carlo over an integrated cost-loaded schedule: activity durations get triangular distributions, cost elements get Beta/BetaPERT/uniform/triangular distributions, and discrete risk events are added as Bernoulli draws (probability of occurrence x cost/schedule impact). Each iteration produces a (cost, finish-date) pair; the scatter of all iterations is the "football chart," and the locus of equal-confidence points is the "frontier curve." NASA policy is to budget at the 70% JCL — the point where there is a 70% joint probability of completing at or under both the cost and schedule targets. The upstream data discipline is CADRe (Cost Analysis Data Requirement), which supplies the technical/programmatic basis and historical analogs that justify the ranges fed into the simulation.

Governing inputs/outputs: inputs are the per-parameter (low, mode, high) ranges plus a distribution-shape choice and a correlation structure; outputs are the full CDF of total cost (and schedule), the confidence level at any target, and a Tornado/sensitivity ranking of which inputs drive the most output variance. The method's honesty depends entirely on the provenance of the ranges and the realism of the correlations — garbage ranges in, falsely-precise S-curve out.

Key assumptions and failure modes: (1) the chosen distribution shape (triangular vs PERT vs lognormal) changes tail mass, and cost overruns are empirically right-skewed, so symmetric or triangular shapes can understate the high tail; (2) independence assumptions understate joint risk — correlation is the most-abused knob; (3) the ranges are only as good as their elicitation (expert three-point estimates are prone to overconfidence/anchoring); (4) discrete, regime-changing risks (a launch-vehicle delay, an FCC rule, a constellation-wide radiation event) are poorly captured by smooth continuous distributions and must be added as explicit discrete events.

**Ambitious read.** Read optimistically, Monte-Carlo UQ is exactly what turns the orbital-data-centre debate from duelling point estimates into a defensible probability statement. The bull case (Starcloud's multi-GW arrays, Google Project Suncatcher's dawn-dusk SSO TPU constellations, Thales ASCEND's EU-endorsed feasibility) hinges on a handful of parameters moving together favourably: launch cost collapsing toward $200-250/kg with Starship reuse, array specific power climbing from today's ~75 W/kg toward 200-400 W/kg, radiator areal density falling toward ~2.5 kg/m^2, and station life extending past the 5-year floor. A Monte Carlo over those ranges does not just assert parity by ~2040 (SemiAnalysis base case) — it assigns a probability to it and shows the frontier of input combinations that get you there. Because several of the upside drivers are correlated (the same Starship that cuts $/kg also relaxes mass budgets, which relaxes array and radiator specific-mass pressure), a correlation-aware simulation legitimately produces a meaningful right tail where space compute is competitive. The method also lets the ambitious case quantify which bet matters most via a Tornado chart: it is almost always launch cost and station life, so the bull can argue the whole thesis reduces to two technology curves that are already bending. Framed as a NASA-style JCL, you can even state a confidence-level budget — e.g., 'at the 70% JCL the program closes if mature $/kg <= X and life >= Y' — which is far more financeable language than a deterministic spreadsheet.

**Skeptical read.** The skeptical space-systems read is that Monte Carlo here mostly launders deep, structural uncertainty into a falsely precise S-curve, because the dangerous risks are not the smooth ones the method handles well. Four objections. First, the input ranges are enormous and partly unproven: launched system mass spans 34-59 kg/kW (Turyshev) against optimistic ~14-19 kg/kW claims (a 3-6x spread), and the radiator heat-rejection figure (350 W/m^2 central, with a 633 W/m^2 ceiling) is T^4-sensitive and not flight-demonstrated — the ISS rejects only ~70 kW over 325 m^2. A distribution drawn over not-yet-demonstrated physics is conjecture with error bars, not quantified risk. Second, the most value-destroying inputs are discrete regime-changes, not continuous draws: the FCC 5-year deorbit rule caps station life at ~5 years against a 15-year terrestrial facility (the dominant driver of the ~17-18x levelized ratio), and that is a step-function legal constraint, not a triangular variable. Third, financeability: a non-repossessable, non-serviceable, FCC-life-capped asset arguably carries a 20%+ WACC (HFW view) versus the 10-15% used in bull cases — and WACC enters the present value nonlinearly, so it is both the most fragile parameter and the one whose 'range' is really a discontinuous market judgement about whether the thing is bankable at all. Fourth, correlation abuse: independent sampling understates joint downside (a launch slip, a solar-storm fleet event, and a token-price deflation shock are not independent), so a naive Monte Carlo can report a comfortable P50 while the real left tail is a write-off. Net: the method is necessary and honest only if (a) ranges are tagged with provenance and confidence, (b) unproven inputs are flagged as such rather than smoothed, and (c) discrete legal/financing risks are modelled as explicit events. Otherwise it produces a confident-looking distribution around an unflightworthy median.

**In this model.** This repo already implements the honest form of the method, and the provenance layer is the load-bearing piece. C:\Users\Ferhat\Documents\GitHub\space-datacentre-model\odc\provenance.py defines a Param dataclass carrying, for each load-bearing default, a central value, a low (skeptic) and high (optimist) end, a confidence level, an adversarial-verification verdict, and a source note — and exposes monte_carlo_ranges(), which returns (low, central, high) triples per parameter explicitly intended to drive a triangular/PERT sampler 'instead of a flat +/-30% on every parameter' (see the module docstring and lines 87-90). That is precisely the NASA-JCL discipline: ranges sourced from CADRe-style provenance rather than guessed symmetric bands. Every skeptic anchor in the brief is encoded there: it_kg_per_kW low=59 (Turyshev), sp_array low=75 W/kg with verdict 'refuted' for the 225 W/kg claim (iROSA), radiator_W_m2 central 350 with note '633 is NOT flight-demonstrated', life_yr capped by the FCC 5-yr deorbit rule, and wacc_space flagged as the 'MOST FRAGILE parameter' needing a 20%+ non-financeable case.

Concrete recommendations to make the Monte Carlo defensible: (1) Sample each parameter from a PERT/triangular built on its (low, central, high) triple, and down-weight or widen low-confidence parameters (radiator_W_m2, array_cost_W, wacc_space are 'low') so the simulation does not pretend precision it lacks. (2) Impose a correlation matrix — launch_kg, it_kg_per_kW, sp_array and rad_areal_kg_m2 should co-move (a Starship-class mass relief relaxes all of them), and rev_decline should anti-correlate with viability; independent sampling will understate the left tail. (3) Model life_yr and WACC partly as discrete events, not smooth draws: a Bernoulli 'FCC grants a life extension past 2032' gate, and a regime switch into the 20%+ non-financeable WACC, because these are the step-functions that dominate the levelized ratio. (4) Report outputs as an S-curve and a 70%-confidence break-even on combined $/kg and life (mirroring JCL's 70% budgeting point), plus a Tornado chart — which, given these ranges, will almost certainly rank launch cost, kg/kW, and station life as the top three drivers, matching the Turyshev closure condition that combined launch+build must fall to 250-1000 $/kg for terrestrial parity. (5) Keep tagging unproven inputs (radiator W/m^2, sub-10 kg/kW) as 'not flight-demonstrated' in the output so the distribution is read as conditional-on-physics, not as realised risk. The existing verdict field (confirmed/partial/refuted) is the right mechanism to gate which parameters are allowed to take optimistic tails at all.

## 5. Parametric cost-estimating relationships (CERs) & scenario bracketing

*A method that predicts space-system cost from a few physical drivers (mass, power, thermal area) via statistically- or physics-derived cost-estimating relationships, then brackets viability between an optimist and a skeptic scenario rather than asserting a single point estimate.*

A cost-estimating relationship (CER) is a statistical or physics-based equation that maps a small number of system attributes (most often dry mass, electrical power, instrument complexity) onto cost. The heritage toolset for spacecraft is parametric: the Air Force/NASA Unmanned Space Vehicle Cost Model (USCM), Aerospace Corporation's Small Satellite Cost Model (SSCM, calibrated for buses under ~1000 kg because traditional models over-estimated small spacecraft), the NASA Instrument Cost Model (NICM, a probabilistic suite giving subsystem CERs plus joint-confidence-level analysis), and TRANSCOST (the only publicly available system-level tool with open CERs). For an orbital data centre (ODC), the workflow is: (1) size the physical platform from first principles (PV area, battery, radiator area, bus mass) to get a deployed mass-per-power m_kW in kg/kW; (2) multiply by launch cost L$/kg and build cost B$/kg to get capital $/kW; (3) add communications and operations terms; (4) compare against a terrestrial benchmark in $/kW. Learning curves (Wright/Crawford form, where unit cost falls by a fixed fraction per doubling of cumulative units) are layered on to extrapolate launch price and per-satellite build cost into the 2030s-2040s.

Turyshev (JPL, May 2026) is the cleanest worked example of the physics-driven variant. His competitiveness condition is (L$/kg + B$/kg)·m_kW + C_link + C_ops <= C_terr, with Γ ≤ Γ_max for communications and a multiplicative lifetime penalty Π_life and effective utilization U_eff applied to delivered compute-years. The governing radiator CER is area-driven: A_rad = P_tot / (ε_rad·η_view·σ_SB·T_rad^4), giving ~651 W/m^2 emitted flux at T_rad = 350 K (ε=0.90, η_view=0.85) and A_rad ∝ T_rad^-4 — so a 1 MW heat-rejection load needs ~1.5×10^3 m^2 even before environmental heating. The PV CER is m_kW,PV = (overhead-adjusted power)/SP_PV with SP_PV anchored at 30-165 W/kg from a spacecraft survey. Closed-form parametric sensitivities fall out: ∂ln m_kW,PV/∂ln SP_PV = -1 (doubling array specific power halves PV mass) and ∂ln m_kW,rad/∂ln T_rad = -4.

Scenario bracketing is the second half of the method: because emerging-system inputs (launch $/kg, radiator W/m^2 in flight, on-orbit life, WACC) are deeply uncertain, analysts compute an optimist case and a skeptic case and report the band. SemiAnalysis's Space Datacenter TCO model is the explicit commercial instance — it builds the full cost stack from launch-vehicle physics and Stefan-Boltzmann heat rejection to GPU-level TCO across 2026-2050, then runs a "Base Case" and an "Elon Musk Case" with user-tunable launch-reuse maturity. Google's Project Suncatcher applies the same logic informally, extrapolating historical launch-price learning rates to a <$200/kg mid-2030s threshold for parity.

**Ambitious read.** Read optimistically, parametric CERs say the only variable that really matters is launch $/kg, and that variable is on a steep, well-documented learning curve. Google Suncatcher argues that extrapolating historical launch-price learning rates puts cost below ~$200/kg by the mid-2030s, at which point orbital compute is "roughly comparable" to terrestrial per kW/year — and that the physics presents "no insurmountable economic barriers" (its TPU survived 15 krad(Si) with no hard failures, three times the shielded 5-year dose). SemiAnalysis's "Elon Musk Case" reaches near-parity by the early 2030s if terrestrial power/permitting becomes constrained post-2028, with Starship driving launch to ~$250/kg (an ~80% cut). Starcloud markets a 40 MW orbital cluster at ~$8.2M over 10 years vs ~$167M terrestrial, and a 5 GW hypercluster on a 4 km^2 array. Thales ASCEND's consortium study calls a 1 GW-by-2050 constellation "economically viable" with hundreds of billions of euros ROI. In CER terms, the bull case is: continuous 8x-more-productive solar, free vacuum cooling, and a launch learning curve collapse the dominant cost term faster than terrestrial power can scale.

**Skeptical read.** The skeptic uses the same CER machinery and gets the opposite answer because the mass-per-power term refuses to fall. Turyshev's physics-floor base case is 29.4 kg/kW for PV+storage+radiator, rising to 34-59 kg/kW with fixed bus mass — and MW-class compute "tends to reside in the tens-of-kg/kW regime even under optimistic assumptions" because radiator area scales as T_rad^-4 and a 1 MW heat load still needs ~1,500 m^2 at 350 K. At ~40 kg/kW, matching a 10-40 k$/kW terrestrial benchmark allows only 250-1000 $/kg combined launch+build, which is 3.4-13.5x below Falcon 9's published ~3,360 $/kg before build is even added. Three further penalties bite: (1) life mismatch — the FCC 5-year deorbit rule vs a 15-year terrestrial facility drives SemiAnalysis's ~17x levelized-cost gap; (2) radiator W/m^2 (~651 at 350 K) is a model assumption, not flight-demonstrated at MW scale — the only flown analog, ISS, rejects just 70 kW over 325 m^2; (3) array specific power in service (iROSA ~75 W/kg cited, possibly ~33 W/kg by mass) is far below optimist assumptions. Financeability is the unmodeled killer: an asset that is non-repossessable and must be deorbited in 5 years carries a high, non-collateralized WACC that no CER above captures, and SemiAnalysis itself pegs today's gap at ~4x with parity only ~2040 in the base case.

**In this model.** For ODC viability, this method is the correct backbone: it forces every claim through a single auditable chain — deployed mass-per-power (kg/kW) × delivered cost-per-mass ($/kg) + comms + ops vs a terrestrial $/kW benchmark, with explicit lifetime and utilization penalties. The honest output is a band, not a number: optimist (Starship ~$250/kg, >100 W/kg arrays, 10-15 yr effective life) approaches parity early-2030s; skeptic (Falcon-class $/kg, 34-59 kg/kW, FCC 5-yr life, financeability premium) stays 4x+ off through ~2040. For this repo, the recommended implementation is: (1) adopt Turyshev's competitiveness inequality and radiator/PV CERs as the physics core, with σ_rad (2-10 kg/m^2), T_rad (300-400 K), and SP_PV (30-165 W/kg) as named, sourced parameters rather than magic numbers; (2) layer a Wright/Crawford learning curve on L$/kg with a tunable progress ratio; (3) add a Π_life multiplier keyed to the 5-yr deorbit constraint and a separate WACC/financeability term (the repossessability gap) that the heritage CERs omit; (4) drive everything from an optimist/skeptic scenario toggle mirroring SemiAnalysis's Base vs Elon Musk cases, reporting the bracket and the single break-even launch $/kg as the headline. The load-bearing sensitivities to expose are ∂ln m_kW,rad/∂ln T_rad = -4 and ∂ln m_kW,PV/∂ln SP_PV = -1, since those identify the two highest-leverage technology knobs.

## 6. Sensitivity analysis (one-at-a-time tornado; global Sobol/Morris) for orbital data centre viability

*A family of techniques that ranks how much each input parameter moves a model output (here the space/ground levelized-cost-of-compute ratio), spanning cheap one-at-a-time tornado sweeps and variance-based global methods (Sobol indices, Morris screening) for identifying dominant cost and mass drivers.*

One-at-a-time (OAT) sensitivity analysis perturbs each input across a plausible low/high range while holding all others at their base value, recording the resulting output swing. Sorting parameters by swing magnitude produces a tornado diagram: the longest bar at top is the input the output is most sensitive to. The governing operation is simply f(x_i=low | x_-i=base) versus f(x_i=high | x_-i=base) for each i; output is a ranked set of [low, high] intervals. It is cheap (2P+1 model evaluations for P parameters), interpretable, and the standard tool in health-economics and engineering cost-effectiveness work (York Health Economics Consortium; TreeAge). Its core assumption — and limitation — is that effects are explored only along the axes through the base point, so it misses interactions and is local to the chosen operating point.

Global sensitivity analysis (GSA) relaxes that assumption by sampling the full joint input space. Variance-based Sobol indices decompose output variance into contributions from each input and their interactions: the first-order index S_i is the fraction of variance explained by x_i alone, the total-effect index S_Ti captures x_i plus all interactions involving it, and S_Ti > S_i flags interaction structure. Sobol is rigorous but expensive, requiring on the order of N·(P+2) model evaluations (Sobol/Saltelli sampling). Morris screening (elementary-effects method) is the cheap GSA workhorse: it computes elementary effects along randomized one-factor-at-a-time trajectories through the input hypercube, summarizing each factor by the mean (mu*) and standard deviation (sigma) of its elementary effects — mu* ranks importance, sigma flags nonlinearity/interaction. Morris needs far fewer runs than Sobol and is used to screen out non-influential factors before paying for a full Sobol study; an enhanced Morris variant has been shown to be a good proxy for the Sobol index.

In this repo, the implemented method is OAT tornado, not Sobol/Morris. build_dashboard.py (the `// tornado` block around line 767 and the chart at line 331, "What moves the answer ... One-at-a-time tornado on the space/ground LCOC ratio") sweeps eleven parameters — launch $/kg (250-1600), space WACC (0.103-0.20), array $/W, IT $/W, ground $/MWh, IT kg/kW (8-40), array W/kg (75-400), radiator kg/m2, T_rad, station life (5-10 yr), and radiation availability — re-evaluating the full LCOC model at each endpoint and sorting bars by swing. The output metric is the space/ground LCOC ratio with a parity reference line. Each parameter's low/high range is sourced and provenance-tagged in odc/provenance.py and the dashboard parameter table, so the tornado is calibrated rather than arbitrary.

The repo does not implement variance-based GSA. Adding Sobol/Morris (e.g. via SALib over the lcoc_and_npv model in odc/core.py) would be the natural next step to capture interactions the OAT tornado cannot see — notably the launch-$/kg x mass-per-kW x station-life cluster, which interact multiplicatively in the launch-cost term C_launch = M_dry * launch_kg.

**Ambitious read.** Read optimistically, sensitivity analysis is what makes the gigawatt-orbital case actionable rather than hand-wavy. Because the tornado consistently puts launch $/kg at or near the top of the bar chart, the bull case collapses to a single tractable bet: get launch to ~$200-250/kg via Starship-class reuse, and most of the other parameters stop mattering. Google Project Suncatcher's entire economic argument is essentially a one-at-a-time sensitivity result — at <$200/kg by the mid-2030s, per-kW/yr cost reaches terrestrial parity — and Suncatcher stacks a second favorable lever (solar up to 8x more productive in the chosen dawn-dusk orbit, removing PUE and electricity-price drag). Starcloud's multi-GW / 88,000-satellite FCC filing and Thales ASCEND's 1 GW-by-2050 viability finding are bets that the dominant variable moves the right way. For the repo specifically, the tornado is the strongest storytelling device in the dashboard: it shows that under the Mature scenario (cheap launch, 350 W/kg arrays, high-W/m2 radiators, 10-yr life) the space/ground LCOC ratio can be pushed toward parity, and it isolates exactly which three or four levers (launch price, mass/kW, station life, array W/kg) a credible roadmap must deliver. GSA would strengthen this further by quantifying the favorable interaction between cheap launch and low mass/kW.

**Skeptical read.** The skeptical read is that sensitivity analysis, done honestly, mostly documents how fragile the orbital case is. The OAT tornado in this repo is local and interaction-blind: it sweeps one parameter at a time around a base point, so it cannot capture that launch $/kg, IT kg/kW and shielding mass enter the launch-cost term multiplicatively (C_launch = M_dry * launch_kg). The true joint downside is therefore worse than any single bar implies — exactly what a Sobol total-effect index would expose, and exactly what the repo has not yet implemented. Several swept ranges are anchored on unproven physics: radiator W/m2 (repo's own note calls this the single biggest model sensitivity; proven floor ~150-200 W/m2 at ISS temperatures, 633 W/m2 NOT flight-demonstrated) and array specific power (iROSA ~75 W/kg is a derived figure, not a primary-sourced spec; 110 W/kg is a spec and 350 W/kg is an aspirational Mega-ROSA target). Turyshev's independent 34-59 kg/kW versus operators' ~14-20 kg/kW is a 2-4x mass disagreement upstream of the whole tornado — wrong base mass shifts every bar. The FCC 5-year deorbit rule hard-caps station life at ~5 yr versus a 15-yr terrestrial facility, which SemiAnalysis shows alone yields a ~17x higher levelized facility-capital charge. And the tornado omits a structural risk it should carry: a non-repossessable, non-financeable on-orbit asset arguably deserves a WACC above the assumed 15% — which the repo already labels the MOST FRAGILE parameter. Net: the method is necessary and well-executed here, but in OAT form it flatters the concept by hiding interaction risk and by treating not-yet-demonstrated W/m2 and W/kg endpoints as if they sat inside the feasible range.

**In this model.** In this repo the method is already implemented as a one-at-a-time tornado on the space/ground LCOC ratio: build_dashboard.py builds the chart titled 'What moves the answer' (line 331) and the sweep array `sw` (line 768) re-evaluates the full lcoc_and_npv model (odc/core.py) at the low/high endpoints of eleven parameters — launch $/kg (250-1600), space WACC (0.103-0.20), array $/W, IT $/W, ground $/MWh, IT kg/kW (8-40), array W/kg (75-400), radiator kg/m2 (2.5-14), T_rad (305-333 K), station life (5-10 yr) and radiation availability (0.92-0.98) — sorting bars by swing and color-coding swings >0.3. Every range is provenance-tagged in odc/provenance.py (e.g. radiator_W_m2 350 [166-633] 'partial', sp_array 110 [75-400] 'refuted') and annotated inline in core.py, so the tornado is calibrated against the cited literature rather than guessed. Concretely it should/does surface the same hierarchy the sources support: launch $/kg and mass/kW (the Turyshev 34-59 vs operator 14-20 disagreement, core.py line 82), station life (FCC 5-yr cap, core.py line 95), radiator W/m2 (flagged as the single biggest physics sensitivity, core.py lines 67-70) and space WACC (labelled MOST FRAGILE, core.py line 105). The clear next step for the repo is to add variance-based GSA — Morris screening then Sobol indices (e.g. SALib wrapping lcoc_and_npv) — to quantify the launch x mass x life interaction the OAT tornado structurally cannot see, and to report total-effect indices alongside the existing first-order-style bars. Recommended but not done: wire a Sobol/Morris module into odc/core.py and expose total-effect rankings in the dashboard next to the tornado, and add the financing-structure risk (non-repossessable asset -> WACC) as an explicit swept input rather than a fixed assumption.

## 7. Break-even & parametric trade-space analysis (incl. break-even launch-price solving, design-of-experiments, and Multi-Attribute Tradespace Exploration / MATE)

*A family of front-end space-systems methods that enumerate a parametric design space, levelize cost and value across architectures, and solve for the break-even value of the dominant driver (here: launch price $/kg) at which an orbital data centre reaches cost parity with a terrestrial one.*

The method has two coupled layers. The first is **break-even / cost-parity solving**: you write a levelized-cost-of-compute (LCOC) or total-cost-of-ownership identity for both the space and ground systems, hold all parameters fixed except the dominant driver, and solve for the value of that driver at which the two costs are equal. For orbital data centres (ODCs) the dominant driver is overwhelmingly launch price in $/kg, because launched dry mass per delivered IT-kW (the mass budget) couples almost everything else — array, radiator, battery, shielding, structure — into a single mass-times-launch-price term. In this repo that solve is done in closed form: `odc/core.py` computes a break-even launch price `L_be` by isolating the launch-cost term in the annualized capex identity (`L_be = (target_ann_capex - A) * 1e6 / (M_dry * (1+insurance_frac))`, lines 240-244), where `target_ann_capex` is the annual capex the space system is *allowed* given the ground LCOC, the space utilisation, and the capital recovery factor. Turyshev's paper does the same inversion analytically: at a deployed 40 kg/kW and a terrestrial benchmark of 10-40 k$/kW, the allowance for combined launch-plus-build is only 250-1000 $/kg.

The second layer is **parametric trade-space exploration / design-of-experiments**, formalised as **Multi-Attribute Tradespace Exploration (MATE)** (Ross & Hastings, MIT SEAri). MATE applies decision theory to model- and simulation-based design: you define a design vector (the tunable architecture parameters), pass each candidate through parametric performance, cost, and value models, and *enumerate thousands or more* architectures rather than optimising a single point design. Each architecture is plotted as a point in a cost (x) vs multi-attribute utility (y, a dimensionless 0-to-1 satisfaction metric elicited from stakeholders via formal utility interviews) plane; the Pareto-efficient frontier is the locus of non-dominated designs, and only that handful is matured in detailed concurrent engineering. The governing objects are: the design vector, single-attribute utility curves aggregated by a multiplicative utility function, a lifecycle-cost model, and the resulting tradespace scatter plus Pareto front. The paradigm explicitly *discourages premature point optimisation* in favour of mapping the relationship between what is desired and what is technically feasible.

For ODCs the two layers fuse: the "trade space" is the parameter grid (specific power W/kg, radiator W/m^2 and areal density, IT kg/kW, WACC, life, launch $/kg), the "attributes" collapse to LCOC and NPV, and the headline output is the break-even launch price and the cost-multiple vs ground. The key inputs are a physics/mass-budget model (Stefan-Boltzmann radiator sizing, solar-array sizing with eclipse oversizing and degradation, battery ride-through) feeding a finance model (capital recovery factor levelization with mismatched space vs ground asset lives, WACC, revenue decline). The key assumptions are that the mass budget is correct in kg/kW, that the radiator's net rejection per m^2 is achievable, that WACC is financeable, and that the asset earns over its assumed life — each of which is contested (see skeptic view).

This repo is itself a worked instance of the method: `odc/core.py` is the parametric physics+finance engine, `odc/scenarios.py` defines the design-vector presets (TODAY/EARLY/MATURE) plus OPTIMIST and SKEPTIC bracketing corners of the trade space, and `odc/finance.py` adds WACC-trajectory and non-financeable-WACC sensitivity arms. It is calibrated to within ~2% of the SemiAnalysis 2026 anchors and brackets the result with the contested-parameter ends rather than reporting a single point — exactly the MATE anti-premature-optimisation stance.

**Ambitious read.** Read optimistically, break-even/trade-space analysis is the method that makes the bull case *quantitative and falsifiable* rather than hand-wavy. The thesis collapses to one tractable variable — launch $/kg — and every credible bull (Google Suncatcher, SemiAnalysis base case, Starcloud) agrees on roughly where the break-even sits: launch prices below ~$200-500/kg flip orbital DCs to parity. Google's own learning-rate extrapolation says <$200/kg is reachable by the mid-2030s, at which point a space DC's launch+ops cost 'could become roughly comparable' to terrestrial energy cost per kW/year; SemiAnalysis's first-principles TCO model independently lands on ~2040 base-case parity and only a ~30% gap by the early 2030s; Starcloud is already funded at a $1.1B valuation building toward 10 MW per Starship and 'tens of GW annually'. Because the method enumerates the whole trade space rather than a point design, the bull can also show that several parameters move *together* and favourably with scale and learning (array $/W, radiator areal density, IT kg/kW, WACC de-risking from 15% to 10.3%, life extending 5->10 yr post-2032). The MATE framing reinforces this: there is a Pareto frontier of architectures, and the optimist argues the frontier shifts decisively toward parity as Starship cadence ramps. Add the non-cost attributes MATE is built to capture — zero water cooling, near-continuous solar (8x Earth productivity in dawn-dusk SSO), siting free of grid queues and public backlash, and (ASCEND's argument) lifecycle-carbon advantage — and the utility axis, not just the cost axis, tilts toward orbit.

**Skeptical read.** The same method, run honestly, is mostly bad news for the bull. First, the break-even is brutally sensitive to the mass budget, and the independent number is far heavier than the bull's: Turyshev gets 29.4 kg/kW base and 34-59 kg/kW with fixed spacecraft mass, versus the 8-19.5 kg/kW used in optimistic decks. At 40 kg/kW the *entire* allowance for launch-plus-build is 250-1000 $/kg — 3.4-13.5x below today's Falcon 9 dedicated LEO price *before you build the spacecraft*. So the headline 'just need $200-500/kg launch' quietly assumes both Starship pricing that does not yet exist *and* a mass budget roughly half of the independent estimate. Second, two unproven physical parameters carry most of the result. Radiator net rejection per m^2 is the single biggest sensitivity, yet the only flight-demonstrated point is the ISS at ~215 W/m^2 effective (70 kW over 325 m^2); the 600+ W/m^2 in optimistic budgets is a coated-edge-on lab ceiling, not flight-demonstrated. Array specific power is similar: iROSA flies at ~75 W/kg, while trade studies plug in 110-400 W/kg specs/targets. Third, the finance is structurally unfavourable in a way the cost axis hides: the asset has a 5-year FCC-deorbit-capped life versus a 15-year terrestrial facility, which alone drives a ~17x levelized-cost penalty in the SemiAnalysis base case, and the capital is genuinely non-financeable — orbital assets are non-repossessable, non-serviceable, and the Cape Town Space Protocol is not in force, so the realistic WACC is pure equity (20%+), not the 10.3% the bull assumes. Insurance compounds this: the dedicated space-insurance market (~$300M max per risk, ~$0.5-0.7B annual pool) cannot underwrite a $10B+ GW asset, so the tail risk sits uninsured on the balance sheet. The disciplined MATE conclusion (Turyshev's) is narrow: space-native preprocessing and comms-integrated edge compute are credible early; general terrestrial-user compute closes *only* under simultaneously low comms intensity, high utilisation, long life, and very low launch+build cost — i.e., everything has to break right at once.

**In this model.** This method is the spine of ODC viability assessment and of this repo specifically. Every serious analysis — SemiAnalysis's TCO model, Google Suncatcher's learning-rate study, Turyshev's competitiveness conditions, and this codebase — reduces the question to: enumerate the parametric design space, levelize space vs ground cost, and solve for the break-even launch $/kg (or equivalently the cost-multiple). The repo implements both layers cleanly: `odc/core.py:240-244` solves the break-even launch price in closed form from the annualized-capex identity, and `lcoc_and_npv` returns the LCOC ratio and NPV for any design vector; `odc/scenarios.py` is the design-of-experiments grid — calibrated TODAY/EARLY/MATURE presets plus OPTIMIST (Starcloud/Suncatcher corner: 400 W/kg, 9 kg/kW, $100/kg, 10.3% WACC, 10-yr life) and SKEPTIC (Turyshev/iROSA corner: 75 W/kg, 40 kg/kW, $1600/kg, 20% WACC, 5-yr life) corners that bracket rather than point-optimise — the exact MATE anti-premature-optimisation stance; `odc/finance.py` adds the WACC-trajectory and explicit non-financeable-20% arm plus the self-insurance reframing, which are the trade-space dimensions the naive break-even hides. How it *should* be applied: (1) never report a single break-even — report a band across the contested-parameter corners, because the result swings from parity to never-viable on mass/kW, radiator W/m^2, life, and WACC alone; (2) flag which parameters are flight-demonstrated (iROSA ~75 W/kg, ISS ~215 W/m^2, Falcon 9 launch price) versus aspirational (350-400 W/kg, 600 W/m^2, $250/kg Starship), as `core.py`'s provenance comments already do; (3) keep the MATE utility axis alive — water-free cooling, grid-queue avoidance, and carbon (ASCEND) are real non-cost attributes that the LCOC-only collapse discards. Recommended repo addition consistent with the method: an explicit DoE sweep that fills the OPTIMIST->SKEPTIC interior (a grid over launch $/kg x kg/kW x WACC) and plots the break-even contour, so the Pareto/break-even surface is visible rather than just five named corner cases — the current `breakeven_launch` scalar and bracketing presets are a sparse sampling of that surface.

## 8. Physics-based subsystem sizing (power / thermal / mass)

*A first-principles spacecraft-engineering method that sizes an orbital data centre's solar array, eclipse battery, and waste-heat radiator from delivered IT power, then rolls the result up into a launched mass-per-kilowatt (kg/kW) figure that gates economic viability.*

The method treats an orbital data centre as a spacecraft whose dominant subsystems scale with the IT electrical power it must deliver. It is the SMAD (Space Mission Analysis and Design / Wertz-Larson) "sizing from power" workflow applied to a compute payload. Three coupled budgets close it. (1) Power: the solar array is sized so beginning-of-life generation, after accounting for the eclipse fraction and battery round-trip losses, still meets end-of-life demand. The repo implements A_array = BOL_kW*1000/(S0*cell_eff*packing); Turyshev's Eq. 91 adds the explicit eclipse-recharge term [1+(1-f_sun)/(f_sun*eta_b)] and the EOL degradation factor 1/f_EOL,PV. Solar constant S0 = 1361-1366 W/m^2. (2) Storage: the eclipse battery is sized by usable end-of-life capacity, M_b = P_tot*t_eclipse/(e_b*rho_DoD*f_EOL,bat) (Turyshev Eq. 23), where e_b is cell specific energy (150-265 Wh/kg), rho_DoD the depth of discharge (0.3-0.8) set by cycle life, and f_EOL,bat the end-of-life usable fraction (0.7-0.9). Dawn-dusk sun-synchronous LEO is the preferred orbit because it minimises eclipse and can drive the battery toward zero (the repo's 1-MW case gives m_kW,bat = 0).

The thermal subsystem is the binding constraint and is sized by the Stefan-Boltzmann law. Net per-unit-area rejection is q_net = epsilon*eta_view*sigma*(T_rad^4 - T_sink^4), so radiator area A_rad = Q_waste/q_net (Turyshev Eqs. 28-29; sigma = 5.67e-8 W/m^2/K^4). The A_rad ∝ T_rad^-4 dependence is the key lever: running hotter shrinks area dramatically but is capped by GPU junction-temperature and coolant-transport limits, so T_rad is held at ~300-400 K. Inputs are emissivity (0.8-0.95), view factor (0.7-0.9), and absorbed environmental flux (solar/albedo/Earth-IR) which appears as a denominator penalty.

Each subsystem area is converted to mass via specific power (W/kg) for the array, specific energy (Wh/kg) for the battery, and areal density (kg/m^2) for the radiator. Adding fixed spacecraft mass (structure, deployment hardware, propulsion, avionics, comms terminals, radiation shielding, compute packaging) gives a total launched mass-per-IT-kilowatt, m_kW (kg/kW). This single number is the bridge to economics: at a terrestrial benchmark cost C_terr ($/kW), the allowable combined launch-plus-build cost is roughly C_terr/m_kW ($/kg), which must beat the prevailing launch price.

Assumptions baked in: steady-state (no transient panel gradients or shadowing), a clean deep-space sink, idealised view factors, and that subsystem specific-performance figures from a spacecraft survey transfer to a megawatt-to-gigawatt-scale deployable structure that has never been flown. Mission life is treated as finite (5-year LEO design anchor), which feeds a "delivered compute-years" penalty alongside utilisation and replacement cadence.

**Ambitious read.** Read optimistically, physics-based subsystem sizing is what makes gigawatt orbital constellations look buildable rather than science fiction. In dawn-dusk sun-synchronous LEO the array sees near-continuous sun (Starcloud claims ~40% higher irradiance and >5x annual energy per panel vs ground), and the eclipse battery can shrink toward zero (Turyshev's 1-MW SSO case literally returns m_kW,bat = 0). The deep-space sink is genuinely cold (~3 K CMB), so Stefan-Boltzmann says a 20C radiator rejects ~385 W/m^2 per side, ~770 W/m^2 double-sided, roughly 3x the electricity a panel of equal area generates, so radiators are smaller than the arrays. Google's Suncatcher carries this to a "compute per kilogram" optimisation, frames the whole problem as a launch-cost learning curve (<=$200/kg by ~2035), and reports Trillium TPUs surviving a 5-year TID dose with no hard failures, removing the radiation objection. Starcloud's worked TCO then claims a 40 MW cluster costs ~$8M in space vs ~$167M on the ground over 10 years, and ASCEND's EU-backed study puts a credible institutional stamp on 10 MW nodes scaling to 1 GW by 2050. The method's appeal is that every subsystem closes on paper and the only stubborn input, $/kg launch, is on a steep, observed downward trend.

**Skeptical read.** The skeptical read is that the method's outputs are only as good as three specific-performance inputs, none of which is flight-demonstrated at scale, and the honest version of the model (Turyshev) closes against the economics. Mass is the killer: Turyshev's independent rollup gives 34-59 kg/kW once fixed spacecraft mass is added, vs the 10-20 kg/kW the boosters assume; at ~40 kg/kW the allowable launch+build is only 250-1000 $/kg, which is 3.4-13.5x below Falcon 9's ~$3,360/kg list and still under even Starship-reuse targets. The radiator W/m^2 is the single biggest sensitivity and is asserted, not proven: the clean-plate 385-770 W/m^2 ignores absorbed solar/albedo flux, panel gradients, transient shadowing, and the 40% area penalty IEEE Spectrum cites from 5-year UV/atomic-oxygen degradation; the realistic flight floor (ISS-class) is closer to 150-200 W/m^2, which can multiply radiator area several-fold. Array specific power is similarly optimistic: the only flown large deployable, ISS iROSA, delivers ~75 W/kg at system level, well under the 110 W/kg "spec" and the 350 W/kg "Mega-ROSA" target the optimistic cases lean on. Life is the other mismatch: an FCC-style 5-year deorbit life vs a 15-year terrestrial facility forces ~3x reinvestment and crushes delivered-compute-years. Finally, financeability: a depreciating, non-repossessable, non-serviceable asset in LEO carries a much higher WACC (~15% vs ~10% ground), and SemiAnalysis still puts space at ~4x terrestrial today. The method is rigorous; the inputs feeding the bull case are not yet earned.

**In this model.** This is the load-bearing method in this repo, implemented verbatim in odc/model.py (ODCModel.power_thermal_mass): array area A_array = BOL_kW*1000/(SOLAR_CONST*cell_eff*packing) and radiator area A_rad = Q_kW*1000/(2*q_net) with q_net = emissivity*fin_eff*SIGMA*(T_rad^4 - T_sink^4), SIGMA = 5.670374419e-8, default T_rad = 320 K, emissivity 0.90, fin_eff 0.90 (the factor of 2 is double-sided rejection). The eclipse battery is sized by DoD (batt_dod = 0.80, batt_Wh_kg = 160) and the mass rollup applies structure_frac = 0.18 over array+battery+radiator+IT+shield to produce M_dry and a model kg/kW, exactly the Turyshev workflow. The parameter block already encodes the right adversarial guardrails as inline NOTEs: sp_array 110 W/kg flagged as a ROSA spec vs ~75 W/kg iROSA flight; radiator rejection flagged with a proven floor of 150-200 W/m^2 vs an optimistic 600+ ceiling that must NOT be treated as demonstrated; rad_areal_kg_m2 corrected to ~6 (mature) / ~14 (ISS today) after debunking the 3.5 kg/m^2 MARVL conflation; it_kg_per_kW = 13 framed as system-level with Turyshev 34-59 named as the conservative independent anchor; life_yr = 5 (FCC deorbit) vs g_facility_life_yr = 15; wacc_space = 0.15 flagged as the most fragile parameter. The size ladder in odc/sizes.py runs this same physics from a 1-GPU ESPA node to a 1 GW constellation and prints both the literature anchor kg/kW and the model's own kg/kW per rung. Recommended next steps for the model: (1) expose the radiator q_net (W/m^2) as a first-class slider with the 150/385/630 W/m^2 floor-base-ceiling triplet so the largest sensitivity is visible; (2) make the array specific-power scenario default to the flight-demonstrated 75 W/kg in the skeptic case rather than the 110 W/kg spec; (3) add an explicit EOL-degradation multiplier on radiator area (the IEEE Spectrum ~40% over 5 yr) since the current sizing is BOL; (4) cite Turyshev Eqs. 23/28-29/91 and SMAD/Wertz-Larson in odc.provenance so the method's lineage is traceable.

## Skeptical / realistic critiques (the bear case)

From a space-systems-engineering standpoint, the 2026 skeptical case against orbital data centres is strong and mostly well-documented. The anchor source is Turyshev (JPL, arXiv 2604.27197, April 2026), an independent first-principles analysis finding a mass-to-power ratio of 29.4 kg/kW base, rising to 34-59 kg/kW with fixed spacecraft mass. At ~40 kg/kW the terrestrial 10-40 k$/kW benchmark leaves only 250-1000 $/kg for launch+build — 3.4-13.5x below Falcon 9's launch price alone. He concludes bulk terrestrial-equivalent compute 'closes only' under very restrictive conditions; only space-native edge/preprocessing is near-term viable. Independent economics (TechCrunch/SemiAnalysis) put a 1 GW orbital DC at ~$42.4B (~3x ground) and orbital energy at ~$14,700/kW-yr vs $570-3,000 terrestrial.\n\nOf the nine critiques requested: five are fully CONFIRMED with hard numbers (Turyshev mass/cost; space-insurance capacity ~$500-600M premium / ~$300-325M per launch vs a $42.4B asset; radiator ~450 W/m2 with ~834,000 m2 needed for 1 GW and no MW-scale flight demo; on-orbit servicing limited to 2 commercial GEO dockings with repair still pre-flight; debris/Kessler threshold already exceeded 520-1000 km with Starlink maneuvering ~every 2 min; downlink capped at ~200 Gbps demonstrated vs 10 Tbps+ needed). Three are PARTIAL — the mechanism is real but the specific number the prompt cites isn't cleanly documented in public sources: the FCC 5-year vs 15-year 'mismatch' (both halves true, but the FCC clock starts at mission end and no source frames it as a direct mismatch); the WACC penalty on non-repossessable assets (non-repossessability confirmed by space-finance law, but no published WACC delta exists); and iROSA specific power (well-supported ~75 W/kg, but mass basis varies 340 vs ~600 kg/unit giving a 33-75 W/kg band, and it's not documented as a formal spec-miss). No critique was refuted. Net: the engineering and economic skepticism is justified and largely quantified; the two genuinely unquantified-in-public claims are WACC and a clean iROSA spec-vs-flight delta.
